For our product designer · Ritual & Ruin

致我们的产品设计师 · Ritual & Ruin

A different kind of seat for a product designer. 一个为产品设计师留下的、不一样的座位。

Most game merchandise is designed after the characters are finished. Someone hands you a sprite sheet and asks for a plushie. We're inviting you to do something else: design the physical form of creatures that don't fully exist yet, and in doing so, shape what they become.

大多数游戏周边都是在角色完成之后才被设计。有人递给你一份精灵图，让你做个毛绒玩具。我们邀请你做的是另一件事：去设计那些尚未完全存在的生物的物理形态，并在这个过程中，塑造它们将会变成什么。

This is a sample seat. The version we share to show what a product designer's involvement could look like. The actual scope, responsibilities, and deliverables get shaped by who you are and what you bring. Treat it as a starting point for a conversation, not a fixed brief.

这是一个示例座位。 我们分享这个版本，是为了让你看到产品设计师的参与可能是什么样子。实际的范围、职责和交付物，会根据你是谁、你能带来什么来一起塑造。把它当作一段对话的起点，而不是一份被固定下来的简报。

The Products产品

The physical object is your medium. A standalone artefact in the world of Ritual & Ruin: not promotional merchandise, not a reward for fans who already found the game, but the first time this world exists as something you can hold.

The product line can be found first, by audiences who have never heard of the game, and pull them into the IP from an entirely different angle. It doesn't require the game to make sense. It doesn't recap the game. It stands on its own and makes the world physical.

What you build, what forms the line takes, what materials it sits in, what scale the objects live at, how many there are, where they sit on the spectrum from accessible to art-object, is not decided. That's yours to shape, in conversation with the collective. The world has a theme, a transformation arc (cute under pressure of ritual becoming empowered and wrong, eventually becoming cosmic horror wearing the memory of the cute), and creatures that move through it. How that becomes physical is an open question that belongs to you.

What physical form uniquely affords物理形态独有的能力

These are the things only your medium offers. No song, no comic, no game can deliver them. They are not constraints; they are why the product line is in this collective at all. How to leverage them is yours to propose. You're the expert in your craft. No one else in the collective knows your medium better than you do, and no one else can tell you how to use it.

Tactility. The holder feels the object: weight, temperature, surface, give. Other mediums can suggest these qualities. Only yours delivers them to the hand. That makes form-to-character fit a real design question. A soft, round-bodied creature finds honest expression as a plushie. A rock-bodied creature or a metal-jointed one collapses as a plushie and asks for something else: a resin figure, a cast metal piece, carved stone. The object should feel like what the creature actually is. A plushie of something that should feel cold and unyielding is a category error.

Function and use. Objects can be integrated into daily life, not only displayed. If the world supports it, the line can include things people use. A cosy game about connection over tea legitimately produces a teapot set whose function is the theme. A world built around ritual could produce vessels, censers, candles, blades, objects whose use is consonant with the world's logic. The holder doesn't only watch the world. They enact a piece of it. No other medium does this.

Material as meaning. The object is the material it is made of. Soft cotton and cold ceramic are two different relationships to the same character. The composer cannot choose ceramic. The comic artist cannot choose vinyl. You can, and that choice carries semantic weight.

Persistent ambient presence. The object is in the room when no one is paying attention to it. A song demands ears; a comic demands eyes; a game demands time. The object demands nothing. It sits on a shelf, on a desk, by a bed, and is part of the home. The IP has a continuous presence in someone's space that no other medium can give it.

The question isn't only "what does this creature look like as merch?" It's "what kind of object would this creature actually be?" and "what does this world ask the holder to do?" Those judgements are part of what you bring.

Your role in shaping the visual canon你在塑造视觉正典中的角色

Visual decisions made here ripple outward. How a creature holds its weight in three dimensions. What angle it looks most unsettling from. How its form changes across the transformation arc. How decay warps a silhouette. What resolves at the scale a hand can hold.

Those decisions don't belong to any one medium. The comic artist brings what works on a page. The game brings what reads as a sprite or at a distance. You bring what resolves in a hand, what holds weight in three dimensions, what manufactures cleanly at a price point that makes sense. The visual canon is what emerges from that dialogue, not what any one medium imposes on the others. What the product contributes is being a place where physical form forces commitments earlier than the other mediums need to. A flat illustration can defer questions about the creature's back, its proportions in the round, its silhouette from every angle. A sculpt cannot.

The first time you and the composer and the game and the comic artist are in the room together deciding what a creature looks like in physical form, that's a founding decision. It becomes the reference everything else is built from.

What success looks like成功是什么样子

For someone who found the product first: They encountered the world by holding it before they knew it existed. The object came from somewhere specific and strange. They wanted to know where. They followed it to the game, or to the comic. The thing on their shelf is now a piece of a world they recognise.

For someone who found the game first: The creatures they played with for hours exist as physical things they can hold. The weight of the world is in their hands. The IP has a presence in their home that it didn't have before.

For the IP: The creatures exist in the physical world independent of any screen. At a convention table, in a window display, handed to someone as a gift, photographed on a shelf. None of those encounters require the game to exist first. The IP has a presence in physical space that doesn't need explaining.

Why This Approach Works为什么这种方法行得通

Most licensed game merchandise fails as a discovery channel because it is designed for existing fans. A Funko Pop of a character you don't recognise is nothing. It has no acquisition channel of its own. It can only be sold to people who already found the IP through another medium.

The physical product line inverts this when it's designed as a standalone artefact. An object with genuine aesthetic design, that comes from somewhere specific, that has something in it worth noticing: that has its own discovery infrastructure. Convention floors. Independent toy shops. Lifestyle stores. Gift markets. Instagram and TikTok aesthetics. None of these require the game to exist first.

The precedent for physical product as the primary revenue medium isn't speculative. Gunpla outearns the Gundam anime. Pokémon trading cards outearn the games and the films combined. Sanrio's Hello Kitty generates billions annually from an IP whose core product is the character as a physical object. The product line is not downstream of the media. In each of these cases, the physical object is the medium people engage with most.

For an indie collective with a limited launch budget, the physical product also solves a practical problem: convention presence. A playable demo and a table of creatures at a doujin market or indie con puts the IP in front of an audience that may never find it through Steam. The product is the hook, the game is what they find next.

What the product's own acquisition channels look like产品自身的获客渠道是什么样的

A cute object on a shelf at a lifestyle store reaches customers who have never heard of an indie game and never will through conventional game discovery. A high-design art figure at an art toy convention sits in a context where the audience expects design investment, strange aesthetics, and limited runs. A photo of a strange-looking creature on a dark kawaii Instagram account gets discovered by aesthetics alone. None of these require a Steam wishlist. The platform and context do the work once the object is there. Where the product line sits on the spectrum from gift-shop-friendly to collector-only is part of what you decide, and the line may span more than one point on it.

The failure mode: promotional merch vs world artefacts失败模式：宣传周边 vs 世界文物

The failure mode is a product line designed to remind existing fans that the game exists: a keychain with the logo, a shirt with the game's official art, a figure that looks exactly like the in-game sprite with no design investment beyond reproduction. That produces no cross-discovery and no new audiences. It is pure fan service with a limited addressable market.

The question isn't "how do we make products from this game?" It's "what does this world feel like to hold?"

The mental model shift心智模型的转变

From: I am designing merchandise based on this IP.

To: I am making the world physical. Someone picks this up and they're holding the world.

Integration with the Game与游戏的整合

The game currently has placeholder creature visuals. The creature designs that come out of the collective (from the comic artist, from the game, from your own work as a product designer) are a co-design output, not something handed down from one medium to the others. Your sculpts of three-dimensional form become reference material. They surface spatial questions that flat art doesn't have to answer. The product isn't downstream of the game. They're in dialogue.

That dialogue goes both ways. A solution you find for how a creature's proportions resolve in three dimensions may change how the game's evolution animation lands. How you handle the transformation between stages in physical form may shift how the game paces the visual change. The game is watching what you make.

The visual canon isn't a separate deliverable. It's the shared language every part of the collective works in, built through the dialogue between mediums that each have different visual requirements. Your sculpts are one of the primary inputs into that conversation.

The following are integration ideas: starting points for what the product and game dialogue could produce. None of this is committed scope. The actual direction gets shaped together. Each idea leans on something physical form uniquely affords that the game cannot do alone.

The un-toyable terminal form (manufacturing constraint as canon)不可成玩具的终末形态

If the terminal form is too complex to manufacture cleanly at small scale, the answer is not to simplify the design. The manufacturing impossibility is the point. A creature that has become so cosmically wrong it can no longer be held in a vinyl figure exists only as a high-complexity resin garage kit in very limited runs, or only in the game and comic. Its unavailability as a standard product is thematically consistent with what it has become: something that has surpassed what can be made cute. The manufacturing constraint becomes a canon statement no other medium could make.

The object as game input (tactility + function)作为游戏输入的物件

The physical creature is the game's input device. A figure with an embedded NFC chip, when placed on a reader near the player, brings that creature into the match: its iconography appears on the altar, its emotional signature in the audio, its lore unlocks in the codex. The figure on the shelf when no one is playing is the same object on the reader when someone is. The product's value doubles because it has a function in the game, not just an image of it. Only physical form can do this. No song, no comic, no game asset can become an input to another medium just by being lifted off a shelf.

Precedent: Skylanders (Activision, 2011) and Amiibo (Nintendo, 2014). Both built the toys-to-life category by making each figure simultaneously a collectible and a mechanic. The figure's purpose isn't to remind the buyer that the game exists; the game uses it.

The companion ritual object (function aligned with theme)作为伴随仪式的物件

For a game built around ritual, the line can include things people use, not display. A small candle the player lights at the start of a session and snuffs at the end. A ritual vessel referenced in the game's lore that the holder uses for whatever a vessel is used for. A blade-shaped letter opener as the kind the lab observers carry. The function the object serves in the holder's space mirrors the function it serves in the game's world. The holder doesn't watch the world; they enact a piece of it each time they use the object as it was made to be used. Use is the integration.

Precedent: Studio Ghibli's licensed kitchenware lines (Donguri Kyowakoku). Functional items styled as props from the films, sold as real-use kitchenware rather than display merchandise. The film's domestic life is enacted in the holder's kitchen each time the kettle goes on. The Wand Company × Capcom's Resident Evil typewriter (2020) is the same logic applied to a horror IP: a functional Bluetooth typewriter that is also the save-point prop from the game.

Integration with the Comic与漫画的整合

The following are integration ideas: starting points for what the product and comic dialogue could produce. None of this is committed scope. The actual direction gets shaped together. Each idea leans on something physical form uniquely affords that the comic cannot do alone.

The object is the artifact the comic depicts (material truth)物件即漫画所描绘的文物

A scene in the comic shows a ritual vessel cracked open on an altar. The physical product is that exact vessel, in the same material, with the same fracture geometry. The comic isn't depicting a piece of merchandise. The product is a thing that happens to also exist in the comic. The two artifacts share the same object at the same fidelity, written together in the same session by the comic artist and product designer. The reader who picks up the comic, then picks up the object, holds the artifact whose absence is implied by the comic's frame.

Precedent: The Wand Company × Capcom's Resident Evil typewriter (2020). The typewriter exists in the game and in the comic-style RE lore; the typewriter also exists on a desk. The product isn't a model of the typewriter, it is a functional typewriter, and it is the save-point prop the IP's narrative depends on.

Material chosen for character truth (form-to-character fit)为角色真实而选择的材质

A creature drawn in the comic as soft, absorbent, folding finds physical expression in fabric that matches that depiction. A creature drawn as cold and unyielding finds it as cast metal, weighted, sharp-edged. The comic's visual language and the product's material language are written together: material decisions feed back into how the creature is drawn, the drawing pulls specific material choices forward. The two artifacts don't depict the same creature differently. They deliver it through the two affordances each medium uniquely has, eye and hand. The reader and the holder learn about the creature from sources that confirm each other.

Sculpt reference sheets as world artefacts原型参考图作为世界文物

The orthographic sculpt reference sheets (front, side, back, internal structure) are functional design documents for the product designer. In the comic's collected edition, the same sheets appear as in-world field documentation: genus classification, stage progression diagrams, anatomical notation in the observation lab's visual language. The design artefact and the world-building artefact are the same object read differently. The product constraints inform the anatomical notation; the comic's world logic informs the classification system.

Precedent: Gorillaz × Superplastic (2021). Resolving 2D proportion ambiguities into 3D produced orthographic references that fed back into Hewlett's illustration work for subsequent albums. The canonical physical resolution preceded the flat 2D update.

Integration with the Music与音乐的整合

The following are integration ideas: starting points for what the product and music dialogue could produce. None of this is committed scope. The actual direction gets shaped together. Each idea leans on something physical form uniquely affords that the music cannot do alone.

The object plays the music (function + tactility)物件本身奏响音乐

The physical object is, or contains, a sound element: a ritual bell, a music box's metal comb, a chime hidden in a figure's base. The composer writes a melody for that physical instrument's actual sound, not for a speaker to play back. The holder produces the IP's music as part of using the object in its ordinary way. The integration is in the wrist, not the file format. No streaming substitute exists; the song lives in the act of using the thing.

Precedent: Prime 1 Studio × Kojima Productions' Death Stranding "BB Pod" (2020). The pod's audio is bound to the experience of holding it, the sound is part of what the object is, not a feature attached to it. The holder produces the moment the game associates with that object.

Colorway and music recorded in one session (material as meaning)配色与音乐在同一段会话中诞生

The figure's paint specification and the corresponding music are produced in the same studio session: the lighting on the figure, the harmonic register of the track, the room temperature, the references taped to the wall, locked in at the same time. The figure does not have a "matching" track; the two were made under the same conditions in the same room. The collector's experience of the object under the same lighting reproduces a fragment of the room where the track was written.

Precedent: Medicom BE@RBRICK collaborations with music artists where the colorway is developed against the music's tonal quality directly (muted, warm, slightly worn; or bright, cold, glossy). The figure reads as the music because the physical surface finish and the sonic surface emerge from the same temperature.

What's Yours to Shape什么是你要塑造的

The world isn't finished. The creatures don't exist in committed physical form. You're in the room where both are still being decided, and what you bring as a product designer shapes both of them, not just what they look like in three dimensions, but what they are.

The physical form of these creatures is entirely open. What scale do they live at? What materials carry the cute-to-horror arc most honestly? How does a stage of the transformation feel in the hand in a way that's distinct from the others without being a completely different object? Those questions don't have answers yet because you haven't been in the room yet.

Your manufacturing knowledge brings constraints into the visual design conversation that the other mediums don't have. That's not a limitation: it's a contribution. A creature that works in physical form at a price point that can reach a convention table is a more considered creature than one that only ever lived on a screen.

The product line's specific story, its structure, how many objects, what tiers, what channels, is not decided. That's the creative work that belongs to you. The theme and the creatures are load-bearing. Everything about how that becomes a physical product line is an invitation.

You're building the physical presence of this IP in the world. And you're doing it while the world is still being made, which means you're making the world too.

For our composer · Ritual & Ruin

致我们的作曲人 · Ritual & Ruin

A different kind of seat for a composer. 一个为作曲人留下的、不一样的座位。

Most game composers get handed a finished product and asked to score it. We're inviting you to do something else: write the song that defines this IP, and shape the sonic world of the game it stands next to, before either of them is finished.

大多数游戏作曲人拿到的是一个已完成的产品，然后被要求给它配乐。我们邀请你做的是另一件事：写一首定义这个 IP 的歌，并塑造它所紧邻的游戏的声音世界——在两者都未完成之前。

This is a sample seat. The version we share to show what a composer's involvement could look like. The actual scope, responsibilities, and deliverables get shaped by who you are and what you bring. Treat it as a starting point for a conversation, not a fixed brief.

这是一个示例座位。 我们分享这个版本，是为了让你看到作曲人的参与可能是什么样子。实际的范围、职责和交付物，会根据你是谁、你能带来什么来一起塑造。把它当作一段对话的起点，而不是一份被固定下来的简报。

The Song那首歌

The song is your medium. A pop song that holds completely on its own and can be found first, by listeners who have never heard of the game. It pulls them into the IP from an entirely different angle. It doesn't require the game to make sense. It doesn't recap the game. It stands on its own and makes the world emotionally larger.

The song anchors to the game's ending, the moment the system stops, the creatures that got out, the ones that will never be born. Not a recap. A way in, for people who will find the song before they find the game.

The song needs vocals. If you don't sing or write vocals yourself, that's a conversation we'd have together, figuring out whether to find a vocalist jointly or whether you'd bring in someone you already trust. We work it out with you, not around you.

What music uniquely affords音乐独有的能力

These are the things only your medium offers. No comic, no game, no physical product can deliver them. They are not constraints; they are why the song is in this collective at all. How to leverage them is yours to propose. You're the expert in your craft. No one else in the collective knows your medium better than you do, and no one else can tell you how to use it.

Time-based emotional architecture. A song builds, peaks, releases over minutes. The listener experiences emotional shape as a linear unfolding in real time. No panel does this. No object does this. The composer is the only person in the collective who can author the experience of an arc resolving inside a span of seconds.

Direct emotional access. Music bypasses interpretation. It reaches feeling before language does. A chord can communicate something the listener cannot put into words after hearing it. That's how the medium works.

Portability and ambient ubiquity. The song lives in someone's headphones while they commute, work, run, sleep. It scores their life when nothing else can. No other medium follows the audience into the moments between things.

Lyric specificity. Words inside the song name things from inside the world. The only medium where a character's voice can literally speak. The lyric is what makes the song attribution-bound to the IP rather than a free-floating mood.

Earworm and re-listen. A song that catches plays in the listener's head when the song isn't playing. Repetition is part of how the medium delivers meaning, and the listener actively re-engages with the same artefact differently across hundreds of listens.

The question isn't only "what should the song sound like?" It's "what does this song let the listener carry into their week that nothing else in the collective can?" That judgement is what you bring.

The Alan Menken modelAlan Menken 模型

Menken's Disney work, The Little Mermaid, Beauty and the Beast, Aladdin, established a mode of songwriting distinct from theme songs or atmospheric scoring. Each song is a character expression from the inside: "Part of Your World" is Ariel's longing to exist in a world she has never been allowed to enter. It works as a standalone pop song, complete, emotionally coherent, affecting. It also functions as the emotional statement the entire film builds toward resolving. A listener who hears it without having seen the film feels its weight without being able to fully account for it. That weight belongs to an arc they haven't lived yet.

"A Whole New World" is a harder example. As a standalone, it reads as a romantic duet about discovery and wonder. With the film, you know Jasmine has been trapped inside the palace her entire life, that the carpet ride is the first time she has ever been truly free, and that Aladdin is offering her this world while lying about who he is. "I can show you the world" is simultaneously the most genuine thing he says to her and the moment the lie is most present. A listener who hears it without the film hears romance. A listener who has seen the film hears the only real moment between them before everything falls apart. Same song; permanently different once you've lived the arc.

"Let It Go" is structurally identical: Elsa's breakthrough as a song, the film as the context that earned it. This is the point. The model extends beyond Menken's catalogue. In each case, the character's interiority is the song, which is why neither the song nor the character can be cleanly separated in memory. That inseparability is not achieved through branding. It is achieved because the song is genuinely about them.

Applied here: the song is written from inside the creature's experience, and the game is what produced that experience. The song sounds like the resolution of something the listener hasn't lived yet. The game is what they need to live it.

The Kpop Demon Hunters referenceKpop Demon Hunters 参照

The contemporary working example: Kpop Demon Hunters (2025). Every song, "How It's Done," "Golden," "What It Sounds Like," "Takedown," is written about a specific emotional state, for a specific character, at a specific moment in the story. Not theme songs. Not atmosphere. Each is the song that only this character could sing right now. "Golden" is the moment Rumi accepts what she is. "What It Sounds Like" is the resolution she sings together with what almost broke her. The lyrics name specifics from inside the world, the way "Part of Your World" names Ariel's collection of human things.

What makes it especially relevant: every one of those songs is built on proper pop song structure. Verse, chorus, bridge, final lift. That structure is not decorative. It is the container that makes the song shareable on TikTok, hummable from a single listen, immediately graspable as a complete artefact. Pop architecture is how emotional specificity reaches the platforms where new audiences are formed. Without that container, even the most specific song has no vehicle.

What the song is not这首歌不是什么

  • Not a theme song. Generic "the sound of this game" music.
  • Not an atmosphere track. Not a looping ambient piece. It has a beginning, development, and resolution.
  • Not a trailer song. It is not written to serve a trailer. Trailers may serve it.
  • Not service music. The game does not commission this song. The song and the game commission each other.

Structural requirements结构性要求

Format: Pop song with a clear emotional arc, beginning, development, climax, resolution. The architecture should serve that arc rather than conform to a template. Must arrive somewhere definitive. No loops. No fades into ambient.

Length: 3–4 minutes. Must hold as a linear listening experience with no gameplay context.

Vocals: Required. Lyrics are essential, they are the specificity that creates the attribution link and the curiosity gap. Instrumental tracks, however excellent, do not travel into mainstream music discovery channels the way vocal tracks do.

Language: English primary. A second language layer (Japanese, or another) is a legitimate creative choice if it serves the IP's aesthetic, but the primary lyrical meaning must be accessible.

Lyric register: Personal and specific. A character's voice, not a narrator's. The listener should feel they are inside a point of view, not being told about one.

Scope: This brief is written for a single defining song because that's the minimum viable artefact. The same principle scales, if the work calls for it, the standalone could grow into a small album (4–6 tracks) mapped to multiple emotional beats in the IP. That decision is made together based on what the music wants to be, not committed in advance.

Narrative specificity requirements叙事特定性要求

The lyrics must reference something that only exists inside this IP. Generic emotional territory ("I kept going," "I survived," "the cost was high") describes millions of songs. That is not the goal.

What must be present, in some form:

  • The creature's body as the site of transformation. Not metaphorical transformation, literal. The character's body changed. They carry it.
  • The system that used them. The lab. The altar. The power that was offered. Not named, but felt. The listener should sense there is a specific machine behind this story.
  • The non-ending. The game's ending is not a victory. The system just stops. There is no fanfare. The song should hold this, not as defeat, not as triumph, but as the specific texture of something that ended without resolving.

The lyrics do not need to explain the game's plot. They need to carry the feeling of having lived it with enough specificity that the listener knows there is more behind this feeling than the song.

The anchor moment锚点时刻

The song's chorus is written toward one specific moment in the game: the game's final ending sequence, when the system shuts down.

Not a match win. Not a single evolution. The large-scale ending, when the lab stops running for good, the remaining creatures are never born, and a few got out. No fanfare. The system just stops.

This moment is currently undeveloped in the game. The song's creative development should happen in parallel with the design of that ending, not after it. The song's emotional peak defines what the ending needs to feel like. The ending's visual and mechanical design defines what the song needs to carry.

The game's ending and the song's chorus are designed as the same event, in two mediums simultaneously.

The music video音乐视频

The music video is its own art form, distinct from the game and the song, native to both. Not a marketing deliverable. A third artefact with its own creative integrity, living alongside the game and the song, not derived from either.

Timing: Planned during production, not assembled post-launch. Key visual moments are co-developed. If it draws on game footage, that footage should be authored with the MV in mind from the start.

Release: Before the game. The MV is the IP's emotional announcement, not a recap of it. Someone who watches it and has never heard of Ritual & Ruin should find the song affecting and complete, sense that there is a larger world they're looking at, and be curious enough to find the game.

What success looks like成功是什么样子

For someone who found the song first: They felt something in the song they couldn't fully account for. They found the game and played it. At the moment the song plays alongside the game's emotional peak, everything clicks, the weight the song had been carrying suddenly has an origin. Their relationship to the song is permanently changed. They will never hear it the same way again.

For someone who found the game first: They remember the feeling at the game's emotional peak, that specific moment, and the song is how they relive it. It compresses the feeling into something they can carry and return to. They listen to it to go back.

For the IP: The song and the game are equated. "That's the Ritual & Ruin song" is an automatically understood sentence among people who know either one.

Why This Approach Works为什么这种方法行得通

Most game OSTs, even excellent ones, seldom pull new people into the IP. Hollow Knight's soundtrack is beautiful. Clair Obscur's is extraordinary. Neither generates meaningful cross-discovery because both are context-agnostic, emotionally resonant in isolation, complete as listening experiences, with nothing left unresolved that only the game can answer. The music lives downstream of the game's audience. People who haven't played the game find the music fine; it doesn't create the curiosity gap that makes them need to find it.

The formula for cross-discovery is: deep interlinking + separate acquisition channels. Both conditions must be true simultaneously.

The Kpop Demon Hunters and Frozen examples work because the music sounds like the resolution of something the listener hasn't lived yet. The song arrives at an emotional payoff without the arc that earned it, the game or film is that arc. That incompleteness is the design. A listener who hears "Golden" without the film feels its weight but can't fully account for it. The film is the only thing that explains what that song costs.

What "separate acquisition channels" actually means"独立的获客渠道"实际意味着什么

A song released on Spotify, Apple Music, and YouTube has its own discovery infrastructure, algorithmic playlists, editorial curation, search, radio plays. Someone who has never heard of Ritual & Ruin can find the song through Discover Weekly or a "dark ambient pop" playlist, feel something unresolved in it, and follow that feeling to the game. That path doesn't require any marketing. The platform does the work once the song is there.

TikTok adds another layer. A song with the right emotional register and structural hooks gets used in user-created content, with people setting moments from their own lives to it, creating edits, remixing it. Each piece of user-generated content is a new audience entry point that wasn't authored by us. The pop structure requirement isn't aesthetic preference, it's what makes a song remixable and usable in short-form contexts. A three-minute ambient piece doesn't get TikTok'd. A song with a chorus that lands hard does.

The social clip logic extends further: a 30-second clip of the game's evolution sequence set to the song's chorus is its own artefact. It either holds as a complete moment or it doesn't. If it works, it pulls toward the song and the game simultaneously from a single piece of content neither of us had to produce. That's the distribution experiment: the song finds its way to platforms and contexts where no Steam page or game trailer would ever reach.

The failure mode: commissioned vs co-designed失败模式：委托 vs 共同设计

"What I've Done," "New Divide," "Iridescent": three Linkin Park songs across three Transformers films, all massive hits. By the formula, this should have produced powerful cross-discovery. It didn't. "What I've Done" is not Transformers. It is a Linkin Park song that appeared in a Transformers film.

"New Divide" was written specifically for Revenge of the Fallen, originally going to be named "Megatron." This looks like co-design. But it wasn't. The song was made for Transformers; Transformers did not change for the song. The causality runs one way.

This is the distinction between commissioned and co-designed. Commissioned means one medium is made to serve another. Co-designed means both mediums have genuinely changed each other. This only works if the game's ending is designed around the song at the same time the song is written around the game's ending. Neither is upstream.

The mental model shift心智模型的转变

From: I am writing music for this game.

To: I am writing a song about this character, feeling this emotion, and the game is where you find out what happens to them.

Integration with the Game与游戏的整合

Because you're embedded in the IP from the start, you also own the entire sonic world of the game: music, SFX, ambience. This isn't a separate scoring gig. It's the integration layer: being in the room when the world is being built means your ear shapes what the game sounds like, and the game's emotional reality feeds back into the song.

The following are integration ideas: starting points for what the audio and game dialogue could produce. None of this is committed scope. The actual direction gets shaped together.

Dynamic music tied to game state与游戏状态联动的动态音乐

Music that responds to the game's specific aesthetics, the player's creature evolution stage, the story's emotional register, and the IP's themes. Every sonic element, music, SFX, ambient sound, is part of a coherent system designed around what the player is experiencing.

The most interesting design space here: music structure driving game state rather than game state driving music. Phrase structure, verse/chorus/bridge, modulating creature spawn density, evolution pressure, or ritual event timing. The player may or may not notice consciously; the music's structure shapes their experience regardless. Whether that invisibility is a feature or a limitation is a design question worth investigating together.

SFX and ambient as unified sonic world音效与环境音作为统一的声学世界

Design both as part of the same sonic logic. The goal is a soundscape where the boundary between music and sound design is not audible to the player. Mick Gordon's work on Doom (2016) is the reference: a score so deeply tied to the game's sonic identity that the boundary between music and sound design nearly dissolves. SFX from the game world become musical material.

The thematic constraint主题性约束

The overarching constraint across everything is erosion: not a mode switch from fun to broken, but a gradient. The music doesn't push the player to keep going and doesn't mourn the ending. It holds both at once: the weight of having come this far, and the specific flatness of an ending that doesn't pay off the way you expected.

Hold these tensions underneath everything: cute versus wrong. Relief versus triumph. Cooperation versus exploitation. Organic versus clinical. Cute does not disappear from this game. It is consumed.

Sonic solutions you find for how the degradation sounds, specific textures, frequencies, the feel of a system glitching, will shape the song's emotional register. The audio world is what makes the game feel like the same world as the song.

Integration with the Comic与漫画的整合

The following are integration ideas: starting points for what the music and comic dialogue could produce. None of this is committed scope. The actual direction gets shaped together.

Panel density as dynamic map画格密度作为动态映射

Panel count per page functions like rhythmic density: more panels compress time into faster tempo, fewer hold a sustained note or silence. A practical arrangement: receive the comic artist's page layouts before scoring a chapter's corresponding track, and let panel count per spread guide rhythmic density. Silent panels map directly to rests or held tones. The comic artist can do the inverse: knowing the music's phrase structure before laying out the chapter, using that rhythm to determine how quickly panels move. Both creators agree on this grammar once; it runs as a consistent rule through the project.

Colour palette as harmonic register色彩调色板作为和声音域

The comic's palette arc, warm and pastel in base-stage chapters shifting to cold, desaturated, high-contrast as the horror deepens, maps to harmonic register. Major-adjacent modes early; dissonant, modal, or microtonal textures late. Rather than the composer deciding this independently, the palette the comic artist defines at each chapter becomes a shared reference for what harmonic temperature that chapter's music sits in. Both mediums responding to the same signal rather than one following the other.

Shared beat sheet共享的节拍表

Before producing final work on a chapter's corresponding track, share a beat sheet for that chapter's narrative arc: the setup, the incident, the consequence, the closing beat. Design the music's structure (intro, build, drop, breakdown, outro) to mirror the chapter's arc rather than mood-match it from the outside. The result isn't a soundtrack. It's two artefacts that are structurally the same event.

What's Yours to Shape什么是你要塑造的

The world isn't finished. The game isn't finished. You're in the room where both are still being decided, and what you bring as a composer shapes both of them, not just what they sound like, but what they are.

The sonic identity of this IP is entirely open: what instruments carry it, what the erosion arc sounds like, how the music handles the shift from cute to wrong. But those questions don't live in a vacuum. How you answer them feeds back into the game's feel, the world's emotional logic, what the other mediums reach for. The composer's instinct about what persistence sounds like in the moment before collapse is a contribution to the world, not just the soundtrack.

The creature designs are not yet confirmed. The first time you and the comic artist and the game are in the room deciding what a creature sounds and looks like in its base form, that's a founding decision. It becomes the reference everything else is built from.

The song is not yet written. The game's ending, the moment the song's chorus is designed around, is not yet designed. Those two things are created together, with you in the room where both are happening.

You're building the sonic identity and the standalone song that stands next to the game as an equal. And you're doing it while the world is still being made, which means you're making the world too.

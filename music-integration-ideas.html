Provocations, not specifications. A scrapbook of the things about Ritual & Ruin that might be interesting to a sound person — written so you can react to them, not so you can build them. None of this is a brief. The point is to spark something.

The actual briefs are over here: Music Composer Brief (scope and tiers) and the short version. They're what this page is an addon to.

Read whatever pulls at you. Skip the rest. Push back on anything. The most valuable thing you could do with this page is tell us which parts excite you and which parts you'd write differently.

AMusic that listens to the game

The game's state — who's evolving, how long the session has run, what's on screen — feeds into what the music is doing.

A1The match has an arc

A match starts as a goofy party game. Cute creatures slapping each other around an altar. Twenty seconds before it ends, someone is a cosmic horror barely held together by their own body. The music is somewhere on that journey too, but where, and what does it sound like to go there?

That arc could happen invisibly (the music gets denser without the player noticing) or as a transformation they can name. Both are interesting in different ways. Which is yours?

A2The lab degrades over time

The single defining theme of the game is decay. The visuals are built to track it across hours of play — cracks spread, glitches accumulate, the world goes wrong by degrees. The music is invited to do the same.

What does music sound like after fifty hours in this lab? Not "the same track but worse" — something more interesting than that. What's the last thing to fall apart? What's the part that refuses to break?

A3Creatures with character

There'll be a small handful of creature types. They're not designed yet. Each one is a little personality — what it wants, how it moves, how it deals with the power being poured into it.

Imagine you got to sit in the room when these creatures were being designed, and the question on the table was "what does this thing sound like." What's the move? A signature instrument, a vocal colour, a rhythm? Something stranger?

A4Rooms with psyches

The lab has different rooms — different environments at launch. Cold storage. The growing chamber. Wherever the ritual machinery actually lives. They look different. Should they sound like different rooms inside the same song, or like different songs entirely?

A5The thing being drained has a voice

The ritual is pulling power out of something. Some kind of otherworldly entity the original civilization figured out how to siphon from. There can be different entities behind different matches.

What if you could hear which one was bleeding into the music tonight? Not on the nose — a different vowel in the choir, a different dissonance, a colour that wasn't there yesterday. The player wouldn't know what it was. But they'd know something was different.

A6The signal you'd want, if you could ask

The game knows a lot of things it could tell you about. How long this team has been losing. Whether they've ever evolved. How close the creatures are to each other right now. How tired the lab is.

Most game music gets handed health bars and timers. If we could compute any signal for you to score against — emotional, mechanical, or completely strange — what would you ask for?

BMusic the game listens to

Flip it. The music is upstream of the game. Beats, choruses, bridges in the song are felt in what the world does.

B1The song as the lab's heartbeat

What if every creature spawn, every emitter refresh, every pulse of the altar were silently aligned to the music? The player can't articulate it but they feel that the world is breathing with the song.

The way most games work, the gameplay has its own clock and the music sits on top. We could do that. Or the music could be the clock and the gameplay could obey. The world stops being a game with a soundtrack and starts being a music video the player is inside of.

B2The altar fires on the chorus

The altar discharge — flames igniting, the beam landing, the moment of power — is the loudest event in the match. It happens about eight times. What if it always lands on the chorus?

The player doesn't know it's been engineered. They just notice that the biggest moments in the game always seem to feel scored. Because they are.

B3The bridge is when the world wobbles

A bridge in a song is the part where the song gets to be a little wrong before it resolves. What if the game's world wobbled with it? An overlay glitches. An ambient anomaly drifts past. Nothing functional changes — the world just has a moment of doubt, on cue.

B4The ending is your sequence

The endgame — the moment the lab shuts down — isn't designed yet. The plan is to design it to the music, not the other way around. The visual sequence hangs on the structure of your piece. Where it lifts, the camera lifts. Where it lands, the world ends.

This is the part of the game where the music is the lead author.

B5The music remembers

What if the version of the music a particular player hears is shaped by what they've already done in this save file? A fresh player hears the clean version. A player with fifty hours hears something different. Delete the save and start over — the music is clean again. The music is a record of the lab's history with this player.

This one's probably too ambitious for v1. Surfacing it because it's interesting.

CWhere the score and the sound design are the same thing

In most games, music sits in one bucket and sound effects sit in another. Here the bucket is one bucket. The reference everyone knows is Mick Gordon's Doom (2016), where the line between score and SFX nearly stops existing. The same instinct, applied to a much smaller game with a much weirder world.

C1SFX written inside the song

The altar igniting. A creature evolving. The terminal sting. What if these weren't sound effects sitting on top of the music — what if they were part of it? Pitched to the same key, landing on the same beat, made of the same materials as the score around them.

The player isn't supposed to be able to tell where the music ends and the sound effect begins. That's the design.

C2The altar as a drum machine

The altar already has a pulse. It speeds up before discharge — a natural accelerando the game shipped with by accident. What if it were locked to your BPM? The altar's pulse becomes a polyrhythm against the score's pulse, builds tension, then resolves on the downbeat where the beam fires.

The biggest mechanic in the game becomes the loudest instrument.

C3Sound effects that decay alongside the world

If the music degrades over a long session (see A2), maybe the sound effects do too. The altar that ignited crisply in match one ignites with a click and a hiss by match fifty. The world is decaying through every sound that's in it, not just the score.

C4The monitoring layer as percussion

The lab is always observing. Log entries scroll, vital readouts tick, tracking brackets lock onto creatures. Right now those make sound because they're UI. What if they were the percussion bed? The lab's bureaucracy is the rhythm section. The track has a kit made entirely of the system watching itself.

C5The terminal-form creature has a voice

The cosmic-horror form lasts about thirty seconds. It's loud, it's wrong, and it's the most visually distinctive state in the game. What if that creature was an instrument — its own voice mixed into the score for those thirty seconds, only there because it is?

Then it's gone, and the music sounds a little emptier for the rest of the match.

DThe small decisions that shape the feel

Some of the most important sonic decisions are the smallest ones — what happens when the game pauses, what fades into what, how the music handles a moment of silence. They're less concept and more taste. We don't have answers; we have your-call choices.

D1When the game pauses

The lab is indifferent. It doesn't care that you stopped playing. Should the music keep running while a player is in the pause menu, like the experiment is still going? Or should it freeze, like the whole world holds its breath? Or duck, or transform? There's a feel for each, and the right one depends on what you want the lab's relationship to the player to be.

D2The fight between music and sound effects

The altar discharge needs to land. So does the terminal sting. So does a creature dying. If they all happen on top of full music, something has to give. Does the mix automatically duck when these events fire — engineering's call? Or does the score itself leave space for them — your call, harder, more elegant?

D3How tracks meet each other

Match ends. The end-screen cue plays. Should the match music crossfade into it, slam-cut, tail out and let silence happen, or get bridged by a 2-second stinger?

The right answer depends on what kind of ending we want each match to feel like. There are matches where a hard cut is the only correct option — the experiment ended, here is the log — and matches where it would feel cruel.

D4When a teammate is down

A team starts with two creatures. One dies. The other is still out there. Does the music acknowledge that — drop a layer, thin out the choir, change colour? Or hold steady, because the experiment doesn't notice losses? Both carry meaning.

D5The first evolution

The first creature evolving in a match is when the game first remembers it isn't only cute. Should that be its own moment in the music — a sting, a modulation, a sudden new voice — or should it dissolve into the layered build? You'd be marking, or refusing to mark, the line between "this is a party game" and "this isn't."

D6The altar's 3, 2, 1

Three flames ignite in sequence. The altar pulses faster. A beam of light fires. This happens roughly eight times a match. It's already a musical gesture; the question is what the music does with it.

Ignore it and let the SFX carry the moment? Align to it from the side? Make the score be it — the three ignitions are the downbeats, the discharge is the resolution? Three different worlds.

D7"Experiment initiating in… 3… 2… 1…"

Every match starts with a six-second typewriter countdown on a phosphor-green overlay. The lab is booting. Should music play under it? Tail out cleanly from the menu and let the match track land on the downbeat? Sit in silence so the typewriter clicks are all you hear? Each is a different feel for what "starting" means.

D8None of the endings are triumphant

Three short cues at the end of every match: victory, defeat, draw. The hard part: none of them feel triumphant. The victory especially. You won the experiment. The lab logged it. The machine moves on.

This might be one of the smallest pieces of music in the whole game and one of the most authorial. It's the moment the score tells the player what kind of game they've been playing.

EWhat we haven't thought of

The four families above are how we organise this in our head. You probably have a different one. The most useful thing on this page would be the part you'd write that we didn't.

A few prompts, in case useful:

  • Is there something a piece of music or sound design you've made (or loved) has done, that you've never seen a game do? Could this game be the one?
  • What's an integration that would be trivial in your medium but seems impossible in a game? That gap is usually where the interesting work is.
  • Is there a single thing the game could tell you about itself — one signal, one hook, one moment — that would make a wildly ambitious idea suddenly cheap? Ask for it.
  • Anything in the briefs that made you think "they don't know what they're asking for" — say so. That's the thing we most need to hear.

If anything here is interesting, surprising, wrong, or missing — that's the conversation worth having. Email collab@playinstigator.com with whatever pulls at you. No format expected.
